## Abstract

The development of Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI) and Large Language Models (LLMs) has transformed the field of Knowledge Management (KM) by enabling precision in transferring knowledge as well as improving decision-making processes and operational efficiency. This paper systematically reviews 58 peer-reviewed publications from 2019 to early 2024. Trust emerges as a key factor that encompasses technological, organizational, and interpersonal dimensions to empower KM processes.

**Keywords:** Knowledge Management (KM), knowledge sharing, Generative Artificial Intelligence (GAI), Large Language Models (LLMs)

---

## I. Introduction

KM in an organizational strategy is essential for creating, storing, and transferring knowledge. GAI and LLMs — exemplified by ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, and Google Gemini — have ushered in a new era where knowledge creation is dynamic and generative, not merely mechanistic.

Foundational theories impacted:
- **SECI Model (Nonaka, 1990):** Socialization, Externalization, Combination, Internalization — framework for tacit/explicit knowledge interchange aligned with AI capabilities
- **Zack:** "integrative and interactive" knowledge strategies aligned with organizational objectives
- **Drucker:** knowledge economy and intellectual property management
- **Davenport & Prusak:** knowledge flow in multinational businesses

### Research Gaps

Traditional KM fails on:
- Managing unstructured and semi-structured data
- Real-time knowledge development
- Knowledge validation, trust building, data privacy, ethical considerations

### Research Questions

- **RQ1:** How do GAI/LLMs improve KM processes?
- **RQ2:** What are challenges in trust-building, ethics, knowledge validation?
- **RQ3:** What are opportunities and risks of integrating GAI/LLMs into KM?
- **RQ4:** What are future research directions?

## II. Method

### Databases searched
WoS (87), ScienceDirect (250), Scopus (9), IEEE (49) → 400 total → 58 final after deduplication and screening

### Inclusion Criteria
- GAI/LLMs in KM, peer-reviewed, English, 2018–2024
- Interdisciplinary/cross-sectoral scope
- Ethical, regulatory, privacy considerations included
- Empirical research with data-driven insights

### Exclusion Criteria
- Non-English or non-peer-reviewed
- Solely sector-specific digital transformation without KM focus
- Systematic/literature/scoping reviews
- Technical focus outside KM

**Final corpus: 58 peer-reviewed publications**

## IV. Findings

### A. Identified Research Gaps (7 clusters, 363 keywords)

1. Team knowledge sharing effectiveness — supervisory and cultural challenges
2. Longitudinal organizational impacts — cross-culture working environments
3. Methodological bias and sampling — data reliability, systemic bias reduction
4. Knowledge validation and transparency — collaborative sharing, quality assurance
5. Technology assimilation and emotional factors — adapting new tech, sustainability
6. Resource-intensive KM — static KM systems, tacit knowledge conversion, strategic validation
7. Supplier development and knowledge transfer — ethics, trust-building, collaboration frameworks

### B. Knowledge Management Approaches

KM is a multifaceted strategy for organizational effectiveness and competitive advantage. Core: tacit knowledge capture and sharing. Key activities: learning, sharing, memorizing, reusing knowledge, optimizing processes, innovation.

KM system types identified:
- **Semi-structured KM** — managing both structured and unstructured data in dialogue systems
- **Context-specific KM** — tailored to specific sectors (disaster management, cultural heritage, education)
- **AI-driven KM** — proactive, dynamic, intelligent systems that evolve alongside organizational needs

KMS four-step knowledge cycle (KB agents): **Retrieval → Reuse → Review → Retention**

### D. Trust Factors (Human Elements in AI-Driven KM)

Trust analysis from 35 papers, two dimensions: **Count of Trust Factor Categories** and **Impact on KM Processes** (collaboration, motivation, transparency → KM creation, utilization, exchange).

**a) Interpersonal Trust**
- Conviction that coworkers are trustworthy, dependable, competent
- High trust → reduces fear of exploitation when sharing knowledge
- Elements: cross-functional collaboration, peer-feedback, team dynamics, psychological safety

**b) Organizational Trust**
- Confidence in leadership, strategies, policies, organizational culture
- Enables sharing without fear of negative consequences
- Mechanisms: equitable policies, ethical standards, incentive schemes, governance structures
- Key concept: "psychological safety effects" (Agarwal, 2023)

**c) Social Capital (Monti)**
- Three dimensions: structural, relational, cognitive
- Quality of relationships: trust, norms, obligations, shared visions, mutual understanding
- Cross-cultural communication and knowledge networks significantly influence globalized teams

**d) Motivation and Incentives**
- Intrinsic: personal satisfaction, sense of achievement → free and consistent knowledge sharing
- Extrinsic: rewards, recognition, career advancement

## V. Future Perspectives

### A. Enhancing Data Privacy and Security
- Advanced anonymization technologies for sensitive data
- AI systems compliant with data privacy regulations
- Emphasis: healthcare and educational settings

### B. Advanced Collaborative Platforms
- AI and blockchain-based platforms for safe information exchange
- Cultural and technology integration for cross-organizational knowledge sharing
- Innovation and IP management, integration of modern technology

### C. AI and ML Development — Reestablishing Trust
- Responsible AI development with multi-stakeholder input
- RAG models for knowledge validation
- Blockchain for safe/efficient knowledge exchange in IoT
- Trust bottleneck: LLMs can produce misleading information → validation is critical
- Key: openness, responsibility, explainability in AI algorithms

### D. Educational Training and Interactive Learning Systems
- Revised curricula incorporating AI and big data
- Specialist training programs for rapid technological changes
- Interactive ML systems adapted to organizational and educational contexts

### E. Ethical Considerations in AI Adoption in KM
- Comprehensive ethical frameworks addressing: bias, transparency, explainability
- Industries at highest risk: banking, healthcare, education (sensitive decision-making)
- LLMs "black-box" nature complicates credibility and trust
- Digital ethics must be embedded into entire KM process

---

## VI. Conclusion

Key identified gaps:
- Need for trust mechanisms, data protection and privacy enhancement
- Ethical considerations and frameworks
- Strategic data management schemes

Proven benefits of GAI/LLMs in KM:
- Enhanced knowledge transfer, creation, and precision
- Improved work performance and operational efficiency
- Real-time decision-making

Long-term implications remain underexplored. Critical for future: maintaining trust, reducing bias, validating knowledge outputs, interdisciplinary research, participatory approaches.

Future research priorities:
1. Longitudinal trust studies (leadership roles, cross-cultural management, trust metrics)
2. Actionable ethical frameworks (bias, fairness, explainability, transparency)
3. LLM fine-tuning for domain-specific applications, reducing resource dependency
4. Blockchain and federated learning for data security and knowledge validation
5. Interdisciplinary educational training programs for responsible AI adoption
